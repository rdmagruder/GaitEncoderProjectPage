Abstract

Introduction: Measuring human movement has broad applications across rehabilitation, sports science, and disease monitoring. Traditional marker-based motion capture is widely used in lab-based biomechanics research but is rarely used in large-scale mobility studies or in the clinic because it requires hours-long analysis by an expert using specialized equipment. Markerless motion capture simplifies the collection and processing of movement data. For example, we developed OpenCap, which quantifies 3D motion and musculoskeletal forces from two smartphone videos. This improves the accessibility of motion capture; however, the need for two calibrated, tripod-mounted smartphones is still limiting in some settings, like the home. If we could accurately measure motion with a single smartphone video, the four billion smartphone owners around the world would have access to 3D motion capture.

Methods: We developed OpenCap Monocular, an algorithm for computing 3D skeletal kinematics from a single static smartphone video. The pipeline first estimates the pose of a 3D human mesh model using a regression-based global pose estimation model WHAM. It then refines the global pose using a gradient-based optimization routine that minimizes 3D joint reprojection error and improves foot-floor kinematics. Subsequently, marker positions are extracted from the human mesh, and kinematics of a musculoskeletal model are computed using OpenSim 4.4. We validate the kinematics of OpenCap Monocular against marker-based motion capture on videos (camera 45° from front-facing) of 10 individuals walking, squatting, and performing sit-to-stand.

Results & Discussion: OpenCap Monocular had MAEs of 5.5° for rotational degrees of freedom and 4.1 cm for translational degrees of freedom, compared to 4.3° and 1.3 mm for two-camera OpenCap. While the rotational errors are similar between OpenCap Monocular and two-camera OpenCap, the OpenCap Mono translational errors are 3 times larger, likely due to the lack of depth information. These increased errors may be acceptable in many applications, given the increased accessibility of a single-smartphone solution.

Significance: The ability to estimate kinematics from a single smartphone increases the accessibility of measuring human motion. Our algorithm estimates 3D kinematics with only slightly greater rotational errors, compared to a two-camera approach, but greater translational errors. Further work is needed to validate the clinical utility of OpenCap Monocular. This work is a step towards regular in-home and in-clinic movement assessments, with implications in precision rehabilitation, longitudinal monitoring, and injury risk screening.
